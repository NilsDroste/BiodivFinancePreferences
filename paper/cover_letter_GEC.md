**Cover Letter — Global Environmental Change**

Dear Editors,

We submit for your consideration "Mandatory by Popular Demand: Biodiversity Finance Preferences in Sweden," by Nils Droste, Jens Christiansen, and Yohei Mitani.

**The question.** The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework's Target 19 relies heavily on voluntary, market-based, and private finance to close a USD 200 billion per year biodiversity funding gap. This strategy rests on a behavioral assumption that has rarely been examined directly: that citizens with sufficiently high environmental concern can be mobilized through voluntary mechanisms at the scale required for collective action. We test this assumption empirically.

**The design.** We exploit a most-likely case logic. Sweden sits at the global ceiling for generalized social trust, environmental identity, and per-capita conservation spending — the context most structurally favorable to voluntary instrument support. If voluntary preferences fail here, the behavioral assumption fails at its own best case, producing a globally informative lower bound. We use a pre-registered discrete choice experiment (n = 2,101, nationally representative) combined with an incentivized dictator game, providing rare behavioral validation of DCE instrument preferences against real-stakes revealed behavior.

**The findings.** Citizens consistently prefer mandatory biodiversity financing instruments over voluntary alternatives. Corporate offsetting and tax payment dominate donation and certification across all pre-registered model specifications. Public monitoring commands a WTP of approximately SEK 1,600 per year. Crucially, the standard political economy concern — that voluntary instrument endorsement reflects strategic free-riding by high-concern non-contributors — is decisively rejected. Both environmental concern and actual donation behavior independently predict stronger mandatory instrument preference, with no interaction (APE = -0.002, p = 0.792), and the study is adequately powered to rule out even small interaction effects. Open-ended qualitative responses corroborate the mechanism: respondents articulate public-goods consequentialism explicitly, recognizing that voluntary participation cannot guarantee adequate provision at scale.

**The contribution.** We develop a theoretical extension distinguishing unconstrained normative preferences (tested in Sweden) from constrained defensive preferences that may emerge in lower-trust, lower-capacity contexts. Voluntary instrument preference, where it appears globally, likely reflects institutional weakness rather than genuine endorsement of voluntary governance as a first-best design. This has direct implications for how GBF implementation architecture should be evaluated.

The paper is pre-registered (AsPredicted #273748 and #287153), has not been submitted elsewhere, and all authors approve this submission. Data and analysis code will be made available upon acceptance.

We suggest reviewers with expertise in environmental policy instrument preferences, stated preference methods, and global environmental governance — fields the paper bridges directly.

Sincerely,

Nils Droste, Jens Christiansen, and Yohei Mitani
